TLDR

— Enterprise AI pricing is quietly shifting from seats to outcomes. Vendors are testing usage-based tiers as buyers push back on per-seat sprawl, reported across several outlets.
— Foundation-model margins are the new battleground. Inference cost curves, not training runs, now decide who can afford to compete, per a widely cited analyst note.
— Regulators are circling usage data. The FTC signaled interest in how AI vendors meter and bill, Reuters reports.
— Open-weight models keep eating the low end. Cheap, good-enough models are compressing the price umbrella for incumbents.
— Procurement teams are getting sophisticated. Buyers now model token spend the way they once modeled cloud commit.
   

enterprise AI pricing

— The per-seat model is cracking under its own logic. When one "seat" can do the work of ten, charging per human stops making sense — and vendors know it. The Information details a wave of pricing experiments where outcome-based tiers quietly replace seat counts, while Bloomberg notes that buyers are increasingly refusing to sign seat-based renewals. The awkward part nobody says out loud: usage-based billing makes revenue forecasting a small act of fortune-telling.
— Inference economics now set the floor on price. Margins live and die on cost-per-token, and a McKinsey analysis argues the winners will be whoever industrializes inference, not whoever trains the biggest model — a tidy reversal of the 2023 consensus.
   

regulatory landscape

— Washington discovers AI has a billing department. The FTC has started asking how vendors meter usage, a line of inquiry Reuters first surfaced, with Axios adding that opaque token accounting is the specific worry. It is a very FTC move to discover the meter before discovering the product.
